🔬 Optional Challenge 2 — The Science of Light
A hologram isn't a photograph — it records light itself. Invented by physicist Denis Gabor in 1947 (earning him the Nobel Prize in 1971), holography works by splitting a laser beam into two paths. One beam hits the subject directly; the other hits it after bouncing off a mirror. Where these two beams meet, they create an interference pattern — a microscopic wave record of the light — which is burned into a special film. When you illuminate that film with laser light again, the original three-dimensional scene reappears, floating in space. Unlike a painting or sculpture, the image changes depending on the angle you look from — because it stores depth, not just colour and shape.
